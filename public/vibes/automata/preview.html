02.3 / UX — Lab journal

Entry № 042 · Re-seed cadence

April 18, 2026 · I. Petrovic · 10 min read

On a 60×40 torus running B3/S23, a uniformly random 32% seed collapses into ash within ~80 generations on the median run. Past that point the population graph flatlines and the lattice loses its character — it stops feeling like compute and starts looking like static. The question we're tracking this week: at what cadence should we re-seed to keep the lattice atmospheric without papering over Conway's own dynamics?

Method

We instrument the simulation to emit four counters per tick (born, died, live, gliders) and re-seed by sprinkling 8% random cells when the rolling live count drops below 60. The threshold is empirical; below 60 cells the lattice feels visually dead even though life is technically continuing. 

// re-seed when colony dips below 60 live cells
if (liveCount < 60) {
  for (let i = 0; i < grid.length; i++) {
    if (Math.random() < 0.08) grid[i] = 1;
  }
}

Findings

  1. ▪ 01 · A re-seed cadence of every 80 generations produced the most visually balanced lattice — neither saturated nor sparse.
  2. ▪ 02 · Lowering the trigger threshold to 40 cells caused noticeable "dead patches" in the corners of the canvas.
  3. ▪ 03 · Of 32 runs, four spontaneously produced a Gosper-gun-adjacent emitter; one ran 6,000 generations without re-seed.

Next entry will revisit this on a 120×80 lattice. Suspect the optimal sparse-seed rate scales sub-linearly with area; if so, larger viewports should re-seed less often, not more.
